# Personal Statement — Draft 2

**For:** UC application + Common App, fall 2025
**Word count:** 612 (target 650 max)
**Status:** rough — show to Mr. Okafor before draft 3

The first time I rebuilt a bicycle from a parts bin in my dad's garage, I was
nine years old and the chain kept slipping off the cog every two pedals.
I spent an entire Saturday on the cement floor, oil on my fingers, watching
the chain settle into the wrong groove and pop off again. My dad, who is a
mechanical engineer at a wind-turbine company in Richmond, refused to fix it
for me. He just kept asking the same question: "What does the chain need to
*see* to stay on?" I figured it out at sunset. The answer was alignment.
The chain didn't fail. The bike did, because I had bolted the rear wheel a
quarter-inch too far to the left.

I think about that bike every time I sit down with my AP Calculus homework.

This is not a metaphor I planned. I came to writing this essay assuming I'd
talk about the soccer team I co-captain, or the after-school tutoring I do
through Berkeley Mentors for fourth-graders at Malcolm X Elementary. Those
things matter to me. But the truer answer to "what have you struggled with
this year" is the chain rule, and I am tired of hiding it.

When my December grade came back as a C+, my first instinct was the same
instinct I had at nine: blame the bike. The teacher rushed the unit. The
problems were tricky. The exam wasn't representative. None of that was true.
I went back through every wrong answer. Every single chain-rule miss had the
same shape: I differentiated the outside and forgot the inside. Once I saw
the pattern, I could not unsee it. The mistake was alignment. I was bolting
the wheel a quarter-inch too far to the left, every time.

What I want from college, more than a specific major, is the chance to keep
making that move — the move from "I am bad at this" to "I see what I'm doing
wrong." I am applying as a mechanical engineering major because the act of
diagnosing why something fails, then fixing it precisely, is the closest
thing I have found to joy. I am not a natural at calculus. I do not learn
fastest in lecture-style classes. I learn when somebody hands me a broken
thing and asks me to figure out what it needed to *see*.

In Spanish 4 this year I picked up subjunctive mood faster than half the
class because Señora Ruiz lets us argue in pairs. In Studio Art II my final
ceramic piece broke twice in the kiln before I figured out the wall thickness
was uneven. I am at my best when learning is hands-on, when feedback is
immediate, and when nobody is grading me yet — when the goal is "make it
work," not "perform that you already know it." I struggle, badly, when the
mode shifts to silent timed assessment with no scratch paper allowed.

I am writing this in February. The AP exam is in May. I have a Tuesday-and-
Thursday office-hours block with Ms. Patel, a stack of redone chain-rule
problems, and a quiet conviction that I will pass that exam not because I am
gifted but because I am stubborn enough to stay on the garage floor until
sunset.

The chain stays on when the wheel is straight. The math stays right when I
remember to differentiate the inside. I learn best by breaking things,
finding the misalignment, and fixing it. That is the engineer I am becoming,
and that is why I am applying.

— J.R.
